Summaries

One sentence

Keshav's three-pass method turns a paper into something you can read, summarize, and reconstruct in three passes.

Three sentences
  1. Pass 1 is a five-minute bird's-eye view: read the title, abstract, intro, section headings, conclusion; jot the Five Cs.
  2. Pass 2 reads the paper but skips the math: aim to understand 90% of the content, write a one-page summary, and identify the open questions.
  3. Pass 3 re-derives the paper from scratch, then compares your reconstruction to the authors' claims to find hidden assumptions and limitations.
Ten sentences
  1. Three-pass reading is a deliberate division of labour between surveying, understanding, and reconstructing.
  2. Pass 1 should take about 5-10 minutes and produces a one-paragraph 'gist' of the paper.
  3. The Five Cs are: Category, Context, Correctness, Contributions, Clarity.
  4. Pass 1 also produces a first guess at the 'read?' decision.
  5. Pass 2 reads the body of the paper but does not yet verify the math.
  6. Pass 2 should be able to write a one-page summary that captures the key claims.
  7. Pass 3 verifies the math, re-derives the main result, and reads the related work.
  8. Pass 3 surfaces hidden assumptions, edge cases, and limitations.
  9. Three-pass reading is most useful for unfamiliar research areas; in your own area one pass is enough.
  10. The method scales from conference papers to theses.
